01 / EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Three eras, one course correction

Honkai: Star Rail is three years into live service and remains one of the top-grossing gacha RPGs in the world, but its position appears to have shifted. The launch and Penacony years (2023–2024) were characterized by record revenue, fast delivery of quality-of-life improvements, and a reputation as the more generous HoYoverse title. The Amphoreus year that followed in 2025 was defined more by friction, as powercreep complaints, criticism of story pacing, and a visible decline in monthly active users after the February 2025 peak accumulated over the cycle.

The Planarcadia era (2026) appears to represent a deliberate course correction — a new Path, a level cap raise, festival-style content, dialogue skip, and heavy collaboration play through Fate/stay night. Early reception suggests it has helped sentiment, though the effect on revenue is less clear: HSR was outearned by Wuthering Waves and Genshin Impact in June 2026 (mobile estimates). A market-performance check (Section 7) separates how much of that decline is genuine share loss versus banner cyclicality and segment-wide normalization.

Core takeawayThe most prominent observed risks appear to be internal rather than external, concentrating in three areas: player trust around powercreep, endgame difficulty tuning, and content droughts within versions. Player feedback suggests that those who disengage more commonly cite fatigue and meta pressure than the pull of a better alternative, which is why the recommendations in this report prioritize rebuilding meta trust and smoothing content pacing over adding new systems.

All figures are sourced from third-party trackers or official statements (Section 10). Judgment calls are marked as assessments.

02 / KEY FINDINGS

Seven signals from the data

  1. Lifetime performance remains strong, though available indicators suggest growth has slowed. $2B lifetime mobile revenue was reached in March 2025 (23 months). June 2026 monthly revenue was $28.5M, down from $38.8M in May — fourth among gacha titles, behind Wuthering Waves ($34M) for the first time. Share analysis (Section 7) shows month-level falls overstate this: much of it is banner cyclicality and segment normalization, with a real but bounded share transfer to WuWa.
  2. Available estimates suggest the player base declined after early 2025. MAU peaked at 30.1M (Feb 2025, official) and was estimated near 11.5M by mid-2025 (tracker estimate). Normal post-peak stabilization, but the slope matters.
  3. Powercreep appears to be the most consistently recurring negative discussion topic. It has featured in CN and EN community discussion since mid-2024, appearing most intense around the 3.0 Memory of Chaos, and preceded the announced older-character buff program.
  4. The feedback loop appears effective when developer responses are clearly communicated and implemented. The 3.0 Developer Radio Special Edition — skip feature, puzzle simplification, buff program — is a documented case of feedback preceding shipped changes, and community response to it was largely positive.
  5. Version 4.0 appears to have reversed the previously negative sentiment trend. Pre-launch concern over fewer events and a "disappointing 3.x phase" gave way to broadly positive reception; some press coverage described it as the most fun the game had been in some time.
  6. Collaborations appear to be an increasingly important engagement lever. The Fate/stay night collab (phase 1 in 3.4, phase 2 in 4.4) is HSR's largest external-IP effort to date, and available market data is consistent with collabs moving spend genre-wide — Wuthering Waves × Cyberpunk: Edgerunners appeared to drive its June 2026 surge.
  7. ZZZ's comparatively weaker performance suggests HoYoverse titles are not immune to decline ($9.7M in June 2026), which may raise the internal bar for HSR to hold its portfolio slot.
03 / GAMEPLAY & CORE EXPERIENCE

System-by-system review

3.1New player onboarding & tutorial flow

New players start on the Herta Space Station with a linear, story-driven opening, then move to Jarilo-VI. Mechanics unlock progressively behind Trailblaze Level and story; tutorials are pop-up panels archived in a guide menu.

Strengths. Low skill floor, story-first hook, auto-battle and 2x speed early — well suited to mobile sessions. Weaknesses. The catch-up wall grows yearly: a 2026 newcomer faces four arcs, several endgame modes, and a roster built around modern kits; text tutorials are forgettable; early jade income feels slow against current pull costs.

Assessment: onboarding works reasonably well for players who come for the story, but it is weaker for players arriving from other gacha titles who want to reach current content quickly. The 4.x cycle has quietly acknowledged this returning-player friction through Quick Level Up in 4.4 and improved catch-up generosity in 4.0. One potential next step would be a packaged "express track" that combines a story recap, temporary loaner units for endgame trials, and a catch-up currency bundle, allowing returning players to bypass much of the initial friction rather than working through several years of accumulated content.

3.2Combat mechanics

Combat is turn-based and built around a shared skill-point pool, elemental weakness break, toughness bars, off-turn ultimates, and Paths that define character roles. In addition to introducing the Path of Elation, Version 4.0 raised the level cap to 110. This was the first increase to the cap since the game's launch.

Strengths. The system offers real team-building depth, and it has absorbed successive new archetypes without major disruption, including break teams in 2.x and follow-up and summon teams in 3.x. Weaknesses. The most commonly raised concern is HP inflation, where endgame enemy stat growth has outpaced older units and turned natural power progression into a sense of forced obsolescence. Community-compiled usage data, such as the HomDGCat sheets circulated in CN discussion, has been offered as evidence that several 1.x and 2.x limited units stopped clearing comfortably by 3.0.

Assessment: the underlying mechanics appear robust, while player feedback more commonly identifies encounter tuning as the primary concern. The 2025 buff program appears to address this directly. Whether it ultimately restores player trust will likely depend on the breadth of the changes and the consistency with which they are implemented across versions.

3.3Character progression & team building

Characters level with EXP materials, ascend on boss drops, upgrade Traces with path materials; Light Cones act as Path-keyed weapons; Eidolons add kit upgrades. Teams run four units — DPS, amplifier, sustain.

Strengths. Archetypes feel genuinely different (break, follow-up, DoT, hypercarry, Remembrance, Elation); the 200-pull Light Cone selector in 4.4's collab structure is a notable generosity experiment. Weaknesses. Best-in-slot support dependency raises the effective cost of a working team from one banner to two or three; four-stars have largely left the endgame conversation since 3.x — a repeated complaint.

3.4Equipment & Relic system

Four relic slots plus two Planar Ornaments, random main stats and substats, farmed from stamina-gated domains. Self-modeling resin, relic crafting, and substat tools arrived across 2.x–3.x.

Strengths. Although the system is heavily randomized, HoYoverse has reduced its roughest edges faster than Genshin did with artifacts, and players tend to raise this comparison favorably. Weaknesses. It nonetheless remains the least-liked grind in the game. Because stat-check pressure from HP inflation makes high relic quality feel mandatory rather than optional, community feedback tends to connect this system directly to the broader powercreep concern.

3.5Economy & resource acquisition

Stellar Jade from story, events, endgame resets, and purchase; Trailblaze Power (cap 240 plus reserve) gates farming. Income is roughly stable per patch, spiking at anniversaries and milestones.

Strengths. Generous rewards have become a defining aspect of how players perceive the game, seen in the free Dr. Ratio in 1.6, free anniversary units, and event payouts often described as larger than Genshin's, with 4.0 press coverage specifically praising pull and material generosity. Weaknesses. That same generosity has created a degree of expectation lock-in, since each anniversary tends to be measured against the last, and the dual-collab-plus-alt structure of 4.4 concentrates spending pressure within a single patch.

3.6Daily & weekly activities

Daily Training now completes in a few actions after being simplified across 2.x, alongside weekly Simulated Universe points and Echo of War runs. The full loop takes roughly five to ten minutes, which is among the shortest in the genre, and the 1.3 stamina cap raise together with reserve power removed most login pressure early in the game's life. The main gap is that mid-patch weeks can feel empty for heavily invested players, and this is the content drought that the 4.0 festival structure attempts to address.

3.7Endgame content

Three rotating challenge modes — Memory of Chaos (turn-limited), Pure Fiction (waves, added 1.6), Apocalyptic Shadow (boss focus, added 2.3) — plus Simulated Universe roguelikes (Swarm Disaster, Gold & Gears, Divergent Universe) and 4.x's Currency Wars with an expanded difficulty tier.

Strengths. The variety of modes spreads value across a wider portion of the roster, and the roguelike content continues to iterate on its format rather than resetting the same ladder each cycle. Weaknesses. Endgame is also where powercreep is felt most directly. The Memory of Chaos difficulty around 3.0 caused visible frustration in both regions, and because full clears reward premium currency, tuning that lands too hard tends to be read by players as revenue pressure.

AssessmentThis appears to be one of the systems with the greatest influence on overall player sentiment. Several of the major negative spikes since 2024 coincide with endgame tuning decisions.

3.8Accessibility & Quality-of-Life

Shipped in rough order: auto-battle and 2x speed (launch) → stamina cap raise (1.3) → reserve power → daily training simplification (2.x) → relic crafting and substat tools (2.x–3.x) → lore-dialogue skip and puzzle simplification (post-3.0 response) → Quick Level Up and instant achievement claims (4.4).

Assessment: the speed of quality-of-life delivery has consistently been viewed positively by the community, and it appears central to how HSR is positioned relative to Genshin. One pattern worth noting is that several of the largest improvements arrived as responses to sustained feedback, which is consistent with an effective response pipeline while also suggesting that some issues waited for community volume to build before they were addressed.

Cross-era patterns

A recurring release pattern can be observed. Each era tends to open on a high-investment story launch, appears to peak commercially mid-arc (Jing Yuan/May 2023, Acheron–Firefly 2024, Castorice/Apr 2025), then softens through late-arc bridge patches before the next reveal. The bridge patches (1.5, 2.4–2.6, 3.8) are where drought sentiment concentrates — and where 4.0's festival structure is aimed.

Alternate character releases appear to provide recurring engagement benefits while also contributing to increasing player concerns about roster fatigue. From Imbibitor Lunae (1.3) through March Hunt (2.4), Evernight and Permansor Terrae (3.6), the Elation Trailblazer (4.2), Mortenax Blade (4.3), and Himeko Nova (4.4), alt cadence has increased across eras. Individually these tend to perform on character attachment; collectively they appear to feed the "roster reruns" perception tracked in Section 8.

Feedback responses have generally been well received, and appear most impactful following periods of dissatisfaction. Three of the largest goodwill events after launch — the 1.3 stamina raise, the 1.6 free five-star, the 3.0 Special Edition — followed periods of accumulated feedback. This pattern is consistent with making the response cadence continuous (Recommendation P2) rather than reserving it for recoveries.

Operational delivery has remained consistently reliable throughout the observed period, with community discussion focusing more frequently on content composition and gameplay tuning than on release cadence. Over 29 patches there has been a single planned seven-week exception in 2.0, maintenance has stayed within roughly five-hour windows, and pre-loads have been consistent. On the available evidence, the operational risks in this game appear to sit in how content is composed and tuned rather than in how it is delivered.

Results vs. market performance — is the decline actually share loss?

Absolute revenue decline is not, by itself, evidence of underperformance. The more relevant test is whether HSR's share of its market declined, measured both against the overall mobile market and against its direct peer set. Public Sensor Tower-derived estimates allow a partial but useful decomposition of the two.

Available market data suggests the overall category is not contracting. Global mobile IAP revenue was $81.75B in 2025, up 1.3% year over year — approximately flat. Based on currently available market data, HSR's decline is unlikely to be explained solely by overall market contraction. The relevant frame is the anime gacha RPG segment and its direct peers.

Reading the table, four effects separate out:

1. Banner cyclicality appears to dominate monthly readings. HSR moved from $8.0M in January, at the tail of the 3.8 reruns, to $38.8M in May, a roughly fivefold range within five months that echoes the 2025 swing to the $71.2M April peak. Because of this, monthly rank changes such as June's fourth place appear to be substantially artifacts of phase alignment, since June fell in HSR's pre-collab lull while the Wuthering Waves Edgerunners collaboration was live. Month-over-month decline figures therefore tend to overstate genuine underperformance.

2. Part of the decline appears to be segment-wide rather than specific to HSR. The four-title pool itself fell from $134.6M in February to $104.5M in June, and every HoYoverse title is down year over year, with Genshin's 2025 annual of $523M sitting well below its earlier norms and ZZZ falling hardest. Because total mobile IAP was approximately flat over this period, the pattern is more consistent with the anime-RPG segment normalizing from its 2023–24 boom than with an HSR-specific problem, and this reading aligns with Sensor Tower's Japan data showing declining paying interest in RPGs. Some spend also appears to have migrated to adjacent segments, since Love and Deepspace holding first place overall represents spending that left this segment's audience pool entirely.

3. Relative to Genshin, HSR appears to have roughly held its position. HSR led the pair on annual revenue in both 2024, at 54.4%, and 2025, and it sat at 46 to 48% of the pair across May and June 2026. This represents modest erosion from the 2024 ratio rather than a collapse, and it is most consistent with the two flagships declining together at similar rates.

4. The clearest apparent share transfer is Wuthering Waves' entry into the top tier. WuWa earned roughly half of either HoYoverse flagship across 2025, at $262M, then reached approximate parity by May 2026 and took the segment's top position in June, while also becoming the highest-grossing gacha game on PC worldwide. This appears to be genuine competitive displacement, and audience-overlap data suggests it draws from the same player pool, with 46% of HSR's tracked PS5 audience also playing WuWa, compared with 50% for ZZZ and 43% for Genshin. The June surge was collaboration-driven, which cuts both ways: it indicates that the displacement is partly a matter of event timing, and it also indicates that collaborations can move share within this segment.

Quantified takeawayWhen the decline is decomposed, it appears to combine three effects: a segment-wide normalization shared with all HoYoverse titles, banner cyclicality on the order of a fivefold intra-year swing, and a real but bounded transfer of share to Wuthering Waves at the top end. On this reading, the degree of genuine underperformance is considerably smaller than raw month-over-month declines suggest, though it is not zero. As a benchmark for the current tentpole, if the 4.4 collaboration phase restores roughly HSR's May share of about 32% applied to a June-sized pool, that would imply revenue near $33M for the month. A more meaningful test of competitive health would be whether HSR can match Wuthering Waves' ability to capture share during its own collaboration months, rather than simply exceeding HSR's May figure.

Instrumentation this implies. A useful step would be to adopt a rolling three-month share-of-segment measure, rather than absolute monthly revenue, as the primary operations KPI, since this cancels out most of the banner phase noise. Churn destination could then be tracked directly through exit surveys and audience-overlap panels rather than inferred from revenue movements, and Fate phase 2 could be measured on share recapture and reactivated accounts against the June baseline. With direct Sensor Tower access, in place of the public aggregator estimates used here, this would become a proper monthly share series with regional splits, which would be among the first analyses worth running.

Best practices worth importing

Kuro's feedback-response branding. Wuthering Waves has turned a visible commitment to listening into a durable part of its identity, and this has coincided with revenue growth. HSR already has comparable machinery in Developer Radio, but it has tended to use it reactively at moments of crisis, whereas Kuro appears to use its equivalent on a more continuous basis.

Collaboration as acquisition rather than only monetization. The Edgerunners collaboration appears to have drawn in lapsed and new players rather than only additional spend from existing ones. This suggests HSR's Fate phases could usefully be measured on reactivation as well as revenue, and the Fortnite discovery experiment in 4.0 appears aligned with that same goal of reaching new players.

Genshin as a cautionary case. HSR's early quality-of-life advantage was defined explicitly against Genshin's slower pace of improvement. Because reputations tend to harden over time, the perception of HSR as generous and responsive is an asset that can erode quietly if powercreep discussion is allowed to settle into consensus.

09 / STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATIONS

Prioritized actions

P0

Rebuild meta trust (powercreep)

The older-character buff program could be published as a standing roadmap item with named units for each version, in the same way banners are announced ahead of time. The 2025 announcement generated goodwill, and that goodwill is likely to fade if the program goes quiet, so it would be worth pairing the roadmap with a stated ceiling on endgame stat growth for each version family.

EVIDENCE — highest-frequency negative theme for two years; proven positive response when addressed (Feb 2025).

P0

Protect the endgame / monetization boundary

One option would be an internal rule that each new endgame reset must remain fully clearable by a defined set of teams from the previous cycle, validated against community usage data before it ships. This matters because several of the major sentiment crises since 2024 appear to originate at this boundary between difficulty tuning and monetization.

EVIDENCE — 3.0 MOC backlash; CN-led pull-pressure discourse.

P1

Build the returning-player express track

The combination of MAU contraction and collaboration tentpoles points to a reactivation opportunity. A compressed story recap, loaner endgame units, and a catch-up currency bundle could be surfaced around Fate phases and anniversaries, when lapsed players are most likely to return. It would also be worth measuring Fate phase 2, which is live now, on reactivated accounts rather than on revenue alone.

EVIDENCE — MAU decline; WuWa demonstrating collab-driven acquisition.

P1

Keep the 4.0 formula, patch its gaps

The lighter tone, generosity, and respect for player time in 4.0 appear to have recovered sentiment, and these are worth preserving. At the same time, returning one combat-focused event per phase alongside the minigames, and instrumenting minigame skip and completion rates, would allow the ongoing events-versus-minigames debate to be settled with data rather than impression.

EVIDENCE — 4.0 reception reversal; ongoing minigame debate.

P2

Ration alts, space spending peaks

Following 4.4, it would be worth avoiding the pairing of a new alt with a collaboration phase in the same version. The cross-version banner window and the selector mechanic were both well received, and extending them as the standard approach for stacked patches would help spread spending pressure more evenly.

EVIDENCE — banner-density discourse around 4.4.

P2

Make responsiveness continuous, not crisis-driven

Developer Radio could move to a fixed per-version cadence that includes a short "you said, we did" section each patch. This would convert existing behavior into a more visible part of the game's identity, and it would directly address the responsiveness that is currently Kuro's strongest differentiator.

EVIDENCE — positive reception of every feedback response shipped; Kuro's growth on that identity.

P3

Four-star program

Committing to at least one endgame-relevant four-star per version family would be relatively low cost while carrying meaningful goodwill, and it would directly reduce the resentment around support-character dependency described in the feedback summary.

EVIDENCE — sustained feature-request volume.
